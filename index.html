Is It Love or Validation?

Mind picks words in a certain way. When we think about those words, we get an idea of them - the idea passed on to us by the society around us through content consumed over centuries.

Words carry the weight of collective imagination. So when we think about love, our mind doesn't just think, it recalls an inherited picture - a beautiful partner giving us their full attention, smiling like we're the only one in the world.

But if we look at this projection carefully, we begin to see something uncomfortable.

The mind doesn't care much about love itself - it simply wants someone to act in a way that makes us feel good.

That act could be anything - checking up on us, saying the right words, giving us time, presence, praise.

The mind seeks attention and frames it as love.

And we end up chasing that attention our whole life, calling it love. We start equating being noticed with being loved.

The moment that attention starts to divide, when more people enter the family or responsibilities increase, the illusion of love begins to fade.

We feel like the other person doesn't love us anymore, like something important has slipped away.

But in reality, nothing was taken from us.

The truth is, love was never supposed to be an act of validation. It was never meant to be a test we constantly pass or fail.

It was never meant to be a confirmation of whether someone cared or not.

It was never supposed to be about changing ourselves just to fit into the ideal template of love between couples - a template built from someone else's longing and someone else's need for certainty.

We force ourselves into this idea of love again and again, losing pieces of who we are in the process.

We try to fit into a model reinforced by a few minds who were also seeking validation for their idea of love.

We mimic their stories, we measure our emotions against theirs, and somewhere along the way we forget to feel what's truly ours.

We lose our lives trying to validate those stories, that content, that imagination, while missing out on the real love that may already be present.

The kind of love that doesn't look like the movies, doesn't speak in big words, doesn't ask for applause.

The love from partners we never identified. Partners who didn't fit into the widely accepted template of love.

Partners who never asked for validation from us, but we were too distracted, too conditioned, too blind to notice.

And by the time we do realise it, those partners become just a memory.

A faded photograph in our mind.

A voice we once knew. A presence we no longer feel. We are left to live in a version of life where we keep pretending to be loved, but deep down we know we're not.

And it's difficult to accept that.

It's a truth too heavy to sit with, so we turn away.

And maybe that's what we've always done - turned away from the real thing while chasing the image.

GenZ: The Burden of Connection and Confusion

GenZ carry a strange burden. They are more connected than ever, yet more confused. They are praised when they perform and ignored when they stumble. They are constantly comparing themselves to strangers on screens and losing sight of who they are. Beneath all this noise, timeless questions rise up:

Who am I? What is passion? Why am I lonely? What does success even mean?

Who Am I?

Am I just the son following my parents' commands?

Am I part of a group where I am only trying to prove my worth?

Am I a puppet working for the desires shown to me by social media?

Am I a soul that gets excused when I hit failures?

Am I just a lost cause in a world that seems unfair to me?

Maybe I am clueless. Maybe I am still figuring it out. Maybe the question does not matter at all.

For most youth, identity does not matter as long as they perform. If they are praised by family, validated by friends, or applauded by society, life feels stable. But when performance drops because of emotional disruption or failure, the applause stops. Without applause, the question of identity rises. That question can send youth spiraling into illusions, drugs, or shallow spirituality just to hide the pain inside.

But identity cannot be built on masks. To understand who we are, we need to remember what we once were.

The Toddler's Truth

When you were a few months old, you were simply observing. You mirrored what you saw. You reached for what you loved. You fell without labeling it as failure. You lived with pure awareness.

Babies carry evolved tendencies, seeds of the soul, that guide them. As we grow, those tendencies fade from the waking mind, but they never disappear. They wait in the subconscious and reappear when we think we are in control, only to knock us off balance. We call it unfair, but it is not. Life is reminding us of the friend we abandoned, our truest self.

That friend, once ignored, becomes an enemy. It resists, sabotages, and confuses us. When we welcome it again with full awareness, it flips back into a guide. That is the same awareness that guided us as toddlers. To meet it again is to return to joy flowing effortlessly from the heart.

What Is Passion?

Passion is one of the most misused words today.

Is it something borrowed from society, or something nurtured in silence until it becomes the only thing that matters? If you cannot sacrifice comfort, approval, or even relationships for it, then it is not passion. It is attraction.

Many confuse thrill for passion. A startup after watching Shark Tank. A job chosen for prestige. A path followed for applause. Thrill excites but does not sustain. It is the same impulse that makes you chase novelty but leaves you empty afterward. Escape feeds the ego. Passion feeds the soul.

The difference lies in repetition. Escape loses its flavor. Passion deepens with every return. Passion survives boredom, resistance, and failure. If you can keep showing up when nobody is clapping, when success looks far, when it is just you and the work, that is passion.

True passion is not found in a moment of inspiration but discovered over time, through adversity, through immersion, through discipline. Slowly, the work becomes a gateway to the self, and the joy it brings becomes an anchor you cannot trade away.

Loneliness: From Void to Awakening

Loneliness is born of self-doubt. It appears when you feel you cannot meet society's expectations anymore, when you feel misunderstood, when you feel lost in the race for resources and roles.

Often, we play roles that bring comfort to others while draining ourselves. We think we are in the right place until the breakdown comes. And then masks fall. We see who people really are when we no longer serve their comfort.

The Path of True Spiritual Growth

TLDR: You don't find God by escaping the world. You find Him by living through it with awareness. Desire dissolves into detachment, pain turns into wisdom, and ordinary life becomes sacred. True spirituality is not escape. It is transformation in motion.

True spiritual growth happens when you stay in life, not when you run away from it. If you think leaving your family and settling in an ashram will one day connect you to divinity, you are living in a deep illusion.

You must learn to work with people you don't like, to face those at lower levels of consciousness, and to stay in environments full of distractions. Despite that, you remain calm and unshaken. Your emotions stay steady, regardless of praise or blame, success or failure. You remain detached from outcomes because your heart is anchored in divinity. With every act of remembrance, contemplation, and devotion, purity begins to churn within you.

This is the only path toward real liberation. To keep working, to keep serving, and to stay devoted while living fully in the world. When your mind stays immersed in God while performing worldly duties with discipline, devotion, and service, you naturally become a yogi. Not by leaving your life behind but by transforming yourself through it.

The Illusion of Desire and the Mask of Spirituality

Most of us carry a deep desire to fit into the template of a "happy life" that society has shown us. That so-called happiness is often built on endless desires. The more you fulfill one, the more impulsive the next becomes, and soon you are consumed by your own longing.

You are not living. You are extracting silent suffering from within, justifying it through rewards that can be showcased to the world. You seek validation, applause, and recognition, but they never last. The moment someone else receives more, the cycle of emptiness restarts.

Even as age catches up and you begin to give back, the illusion doesn't die. It simply takes on a new face. You feel noble in your service, but your goodness still seeks identity. You are still acting from self-image. You have not transcended. You have only shifted form. Desires have now taken the shape of spirituality, and that illusion is harder to detect because it wears the mask of holiness.

True spirituality begins only when this mask falls, when you act not from identity but from surrender.

The Mind, the Shadow, and the Awakening Within

For those who have conquered the mind, it is their friend. For those who haven't, it becomes their enemy. The mind whispers temptations of gain and recognition, and you follow. It doesn't act alone. It is shaped by a collective field of minds, each chasing similar desires. You get caught in that unseen web, pulled by emotions and illusions.

Only when the intellect begins to reason with the mind does transformation begin. When knowledge becomes your guide, the ego starts to dissolve. You stop taking pride in achievement and start taking joy in understanding. You act without clinging, perform without seeking, and live in non-action. And in non-action, there is peace because you realize you were never the doer in the first place.
